    • What is a First-Lien HELOC?

    A plain-language explainer of first-lien home equity lines of credit — what they are, how they differ from more common second-lien HELOCs, and what to think about before opening one. This page is general education, not financial or legal advice.

    What is a first-lien HELOC?

    A lien is a lender's legal claim against your property that secures a debt. When you take out a mortgage to buy a home, the lender records a lien against the property; if you stop paying, that lien gives the lender the right to force a sale to recover what's owed. A HELOC (home equity line of credit) is a revolving line of credit secured by your home, similar in spirit to a credit card but backed by your equity instead of being unsecured.

    Most HELOCs are second-lien loans: the homeowner already has a first mortgage, and the HELOC sits behind it in priority. A first-lien HELOC is different — it's structured to be the primary loan on the property, either replacing an existing mortgage entirely or serving as the only lien when the home is owned outright. Because it holds first position, it carries the lender's strongest claim, ahead of any other loan against the home.

    First-lien vs. second-lien HELOCs

    Lien position
    A first-lien HELOC is the only or primary loan against the home. A second-lien HELOC sits behind an existing first mortgage.
    What happens to the existing mortgage
    Opening a first-lien HELOC typically means paying off and replacing an existing first mortgage (similar to a cash-out refinance), or opening the HELOC on a home with no existing mortgage. A second-lien HELOC leaves the existing mortgage untouched and adds a separate line behind it.
    Risk to the lender (and pricing)
    Because a first-lien position gets paid first in a foreclosure, first-lien HELOCs are lower risk to the lender than second-lien HELOCs. That can translate into different rate and underwriting terms, though offerings vary a lot by lender.
    Availability
    Second-lien HELOCs are far more common and widely offered. First-lien HELOCs are a newer, less standardized product and aren't available from every lender.

    How it works

    Like any HELOC, a first-lien HELOC gives you a credit limit based on your home's equity that you can draw from as needed, rather than receiving a single lump sum like a traditional mortgage. You typically pay interest only on the amount you've drawn, and the line can often be reused as you pay it down during a "draw period," before converting to a repayment schedule.

    Because it replaces your first mortgage, moving to a first-lien HELOC usually involves closing costs and underwriting similar to a refinance — appraisal, title work, and a new loan closing — rather than the lighter process typical of adding a second-lien HELOC on top of an existing mortgage.

    What to weigh before opening one

    • You're giving up your current mortgage terms. If your existing first mortgage has a low fixed rate, replacing it with a variable-rate HELOC could mean trading a known, stable payment for one that moves with the market.
    • Your home secures the debt. As with any HELOC or mortgage, missed payments put your home at risk of foreclosure.
    • Rates are usually variable. Draws and balances typically accrue interest at a variable rate, so payments can rise if rates rise.
    • Fewer lenders offer them. Shop around and compare terms carefully, since this product isn't as standardized as a traditional mortgage or a second-lien HELOC.
    • Get independent advice. A mortgage broker, financial advisor, or housing counselor can help you compare a first-lien HELOC against alternatives like a cash-out refinance, a second-lien HELOC, or a home equity loan for your specific situation.
